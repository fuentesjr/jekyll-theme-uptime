On watching the billing graph during a launch

The first $10M of ARR moves differently than the second. Notes from the day Copilot for Individuals shipped.

by Salvador Fuentes Jr. · from Irvine, CA 6 min read·1,240 words

Launch days are really two launches — the one you announce and the one you watch. The first is press releases and blog posts. The second is a Datadog dashboard, three teammates on a video call, and a Slack channel that nobody quite knows how to mute.

When we shipped billing for Copilot for Individuals, the most useful thing in the room wasn't a runbook. It was a chart. One chart, refreshing every few seconds, showing the rate of successful subscription creates against failed ones. Everything else — error budgets, capacity plans, migration status — had already done its job in the days before. On the day, what mattered was whether the green line kept going up and the red line stayed flat.

What the graph taught me

Three things, in order of how long they took to sink in.

First, a slow graph is a healthy graph. I kept wanting to see a hockey stick. The hockey stick arrived later, and mostly from somewhere else — marketing, a reviewer, a tweet.

Second, the error you're looking for is the one you didn't plan for. We had runbooks for payment-method rejections, currency mismatches, Zuora retries. What we didn't have was a runbook for a small percentage of accounts that had existed long enough to have three expired payment instruments on file from a previous product line.

Every billing bug is eventually a race condition, a legacy code path, or both.

A quick note on gh-ost

One piece of pre-launch work that paid off immediately: an online schema change via gh-ost on the billing MySQL cluster, combined with a custom reclamation job that outran ingest through the migration window.

$ gh-ost \
  --host=billing-primary.internal \
  --database=billing \
  --table=subscriptions \
  --alter="ADD COLUMN product_family VARCHAR(32) NULL" \
  --execute

What I'd do again

  • Have one chart that everyone agrees is the chart.
  • Write the runbook for the thing you think won't happen.
  • Put Growth, Support, Data and Eng on the same call, not different ones.
  • Buy yourself database runway before you need it, not after.

The rest is standard. Breathe, refresh the graph, trust the people you work with.
